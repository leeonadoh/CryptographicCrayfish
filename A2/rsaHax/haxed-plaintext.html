Reasoning

By looking at the public exponent value e = 65537, vulnerabilities introduced by small public exponents can be eliminated.

Small private exponent vulnerabilities can also be eliminated, since e*d mod phi(n) = 1 implies e*d must be at least as big as phi(n) + 1. Since n is a multiple of two primes (p, q), and n is huge, phi(n) = (p-1)(q-1) will also be huge. Therefore, phi(n) / e = d will not be as big as phi(n), but still huge in comparison with e.

Common modulus vulnerability was eliminated by running gcd(n1, n2) = 1, where n1 is the modulus for mathlab port 41414, and n2 for mathlab port 28004. This means they do not share a common prime factor.

This leave two other "semi brute force" approaches:

  1. Start testing primes from the square root of modulus n to abuse the balanced factors vulnerability, or
  2. Run a prime factoring algorithm to abuse the unbalanced factors vulnerability.
We eliminated 1 by a stroke of luck, where our algorithm for 2 successfully factorized n before we could code anything for 1. We ran both Pollard and Brent's rho algorithm, in addition to a combination of rho + ecm (elliptic curve factorization method), where rho + ecm finally won the race.
